# Design-Centering Styles

[[customer-centered-design]] is best understood by contrast with the competing approaches it's positioned against, each named for what actually drives its decisions instead of the customer.

## User-centered design

The closest relative: an approach to engineering useful, usable computer systems pioneered in the 1980s. [[customer-centered-design]] builds on it but goes further, fusing usability concerns with marketing/business concerns — e.g. balancing business goals, marketing goals, usability goals, and customer-experience goals together, since on the Web the harder problem isn't getting an audience (easy) but converting visitors into customers and bringing them back (hard), unlike shrink-wrapped software a customer already bought before using.

## Company-centered design

The needs and interests of the company — its internal structure, its product taxonomy, its vocabulary — dominate the site instead of customer needs. Two characteristic failure modes:

- **Brochureware**: sites organized around internal corporate structure with sparse real information about products/services, ignoring what the Web as a medium can actually do. Tolerable only as a short-lived first step toward something more useful, never as an end state.
- **Insider jargon**: assuming customers share the company's internal vocabulary. Worked example: a buyer shopping for a digital camera who just wanted to know "which camera is best for an amateur," confronted instead with unexplained terms like CCDs, FireWire, and uncompressed TIFF mode — confusing and alienating rather than informative.

## Technology-centered design

Sites built around whatever technology is novel or available, with little up-front research into business or customer needs — animation, audio, video, and streaming for their own sake. Treats technology as the end rather than a means to an end. Was common in the Web's early days and has become less common as the medium matured, but the failure mode (a flashy, slow, hard-to-use site) recurs whenever a new technology trend appears.

## Designer-centered design

Also called *ego-centered design*: the creative and expressive needs of the design team take priority over the needs of customers — captured by the (real, quoted) attitude "the less they talk to us, the better it is. We know what's best." Can produce sites that are technically and visually impressive, sometimes deliberately so as a brand statement, but the slow downloads, poor cross-browser support, and learning curve that often come with that focus are a poor fit for any e-commerce or informational site whose business depends on repeat visitors. More tolerable for art-oriented sites where the visual statement *is* the point.

## Why the contrast matters

Each of the three non-customer-centered styles inverts the same relationship: company-centered design gives no thought to why a customer is visiting or what they want to do; technology-centered design treats technology as an end instead of a tool; designer-centered design subordinates customer needs to the design team's expressive needs. Customer-centered design (and user-centered design before it) flips this: customers and their tasks come first, and technology is one of several tools used to serve them.
