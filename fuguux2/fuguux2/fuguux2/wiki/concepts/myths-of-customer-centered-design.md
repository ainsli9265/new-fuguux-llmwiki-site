# Myths of Customer-Centered Design

Nine common objections organizations raise against investing in [[customer-centered-design]], each rebutted:

1. **"Good design is just common sense."** If that were true, bad sites wouldn't be so common. Design teams aren't their customers — they know too much about how the site works internally to see it the way a newcomer does — and intuition alone reliably fails to predict real customer behavior.
2. **"Only experts create good designs."** Experts apply the techniques faster and more rigorously, but the techniques themselves — observing, talking to, and getting feedback from customers — are learnable by anyone willing to do the work.
3. **"Interfaces can be redesigned right before launch."** This assumes the site already has the right features, built correctly — two risky, often-false assumptions that get far more expensive to fix the closer a project is to completion (see the cost argument in [[iterative-design]]). Constant feedback throughout the project, not a pre-launch scramble, is what actually de-risks this.
4. **"Good design takes too long and costs too much."** It adds real up-front cost (talking to customers, prototyping, iterating), but it reduces back-end costs (help-desk load, returns, ongoing maintenance from customer dissatisfaction) and can lower total project cost by catching problems while they're still cheap to fix.
5. **"Good design is just cool graphics."** Aesthetics matter (good visuals communicate how to use an interface and set an impression) but are only one part of what a design needs to address — customer goals, understanding, tasks, and context all sit outside what visuals alone can fix.
6. **"Web interface guidelines will guide you to good design."** Guidelines are a useful checklist against minor implementation problems, but they don't address what features a site needs, how it should be organized, or how pages should flow into each other — and they don't help with trade-offs the way customer-centered principles, processes, and patterns do.
7. **"Customers can always rely on documentation and help."** Customers have little patience to read documentation just to use a Web site; help pages are a last resort for an already-frustrated customer, not a substitute for a usable design. [[trust-and-credibility]] makes the same point from the other direction: clear, honest content earns more trust than a fallback explanation customers have to go dig for.
8. **"Market research reveals all customer needs."** Market research captures attitudes and stated intentions well but doesn't reliably predict actual behavior, and turning research straight into a feature checklist risks cluttering the design with requests that satisfy individual askers while hurting the experience for everyone else. What customers *say* needs to be balanced against direct observation of what they *do*.
9. **"Quality assurance groups ensure Web sites work well."** QA is technology-driven — it verifies the build matches the spec — not customer-driven, and it typically only happens after the site is built, when it's too late to fix design (not just code) mistakes. It catches coding errors, not design errors.

A companion list in the source, **"Top Ten Signs That Things Are Going Badly,"** collects the verbal tells of an organization falling into these myths in practice — e.g. "We don't need user testing, I'm a user and I find it easy to use," "We'll just put an 'Under Construction' sign there," and "We need a splash screen."
