# Affordance (Wikipedia)

An encyclopedia article on affordance: the psychology term for what an environment offers an actor, its 1988 narrowing for design/HCI use by Don Norman, and the surrounding terminology (signifiers, perceived/hidden/false affordances). Wikipedia articles are continuously edited, so `year` above reflects the access date (2026-06-25), not a fixed publication year.

## Key points

- James J. Gibson coined "affordance" in his 1966 book *The Senses Considered as Perceptual Systems*; his most-cited definition is from *The Ecological Approach to Visual Perception* (1979): "the affordances of the environment are what it offers the animal, what it provides or furnishes, either for good or ill." Gibson's affordances are relational (depend on the actor's current capabilities) and objective — they exist whether or not the actor perceives them, e.g. a chair objectively affords throwing as well as sitting.
- In 1988, Donald Norman narrowed the term in an HCI context to only those action possibilities an actor can *readily perceive* — popularized via *The Design of Everyday Things* and now the dominant usage in design/HCI, distinct from (and often conflated with) Gibson's broader original sense.
- Norman later judged this narrowing unintended; his 2013 revision of the same book introduced **signifiers** to separate the two jobs: "Affordances determine what actions are possible. Signifiers communicate where the action should take place. We need both."
- William Gaver's three-way split: **perceivable** affordances (apparent to the actor), **hidden** affordances (real, but not apparent — e.g. a shoe can pry a cork from a wine bottle, but this isn't visible from looking at a shoe), and **false** affordances (apparent, but with no real function — e.g. a placebo button).
- Worked examples: a door knob's shape as a perceivable affordance of how it's used; tea cups/teapots shaped to suggest their function; graphical user interfaces, where affordance is named as one of several design principles.
- The term has drifted toward an uncountable noun describing ease of discoverability generally ("this button has good affordance"), with "afford" coming to mean "suggest" or "invite" — a usage shift the article flags as inconsistent even across HCI textbooks.
- Jenny L. Davis's 2016/2020 "mechanisms and conditions" framework reframes the question from "what does a technology afford" to "how does it afford, for whom, under what circumstances" — technologies can request, demand, encourage, discourage, refuse, or allow an action depending on a user's perception, dexterity, and cultural/institutional standing.
- Cross-disciplinary use noted beyond design/HCI: criminology, industrial design, instructional design, STS, sports science, AI, and fire-safety signage/evacuation design.
