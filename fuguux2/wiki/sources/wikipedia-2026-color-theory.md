# Color Theory (Wikipedia)

An encyclopedia article on color theory: the historical, largely artistic body of knowledge about color mixing, contrast, harmony, schemes, and symbolism, distinguished in the article from the more objective discipline of color science. Wikipedia articles are continuously edited, so `year` above reflects the access date (2026-06-25), not a fixed publication year.

## Key points

- Defines color theory as covering color mixing, contrast effects, harmony, schemes, and symbolism — subjective/artistic, as opposed to color science's objective/functional focus (chemistry, astronomy, color reproduction).
- Traces a long history (Aristotle, Bharata's *Nāṭya Shāstra*, several medieval Islamic and European scholars, Newton, Goethe's 1810 *Theory of Colours*, Chevreul's 1839 Law of Simultaneous Color Contrast, Young–Helmholtz trichromatic theory, Munsell's 1905 system) — named but not elaborated on beyond a sentence each.
- **Color mixing**: additive (RGB, light-based — screens) vs. subtractive (CMYK, ink/pigment-based — print); CMYK is economical but structurally weak at reproducing orange and some purples.
- **Color schemes**: complementary (opposite hues, cancel to achromatic, strong contrast), analogous (adjacent hues, monochromatic feel), split-complementary (a softened complementary pair), and triadic (three equidistant hues) — all derived from the color wheel.
- **Color contrast** (Chevreul's three types): simultaneous (side-by-side hues alter each other's apparent color), successive (afterimage on a neutral background), mixed (afterimage cast onto a different color).
- **Warm vs. cool**: a centuries-old distinction (warm = red-yellow, associated with daylight/advancing; cool = blue-green-violet, associated with overcast/receding), with the caveat that the psychological effect may really be driven by warm pigments' typically higher saturation and lighter value, not hue itself.
- **Color harmony**: a 21st-century model treats it as a function of the colors involved *and* individual differences, cultural experience, context, perceptual effects, and time-bound trends — and notes that with ~2.3 million perceivable colors, the combination space is effectively infinite, making any fixed predictive harmony formula "fundamentally unsound."
- **Color symbolism**: culture- and context-bound, not universal or intrinsic (red alone connotes excitement, danger, luck, romance, femininity depending on context) — and color's claimed therapeutic properties have no real evidential support.
- Brief, generic mentions of camouflage, heat-dispersing paint, and theme-park aesthetics (Disney) as "functional applications" — one sentence, no design-specific elaboration.
