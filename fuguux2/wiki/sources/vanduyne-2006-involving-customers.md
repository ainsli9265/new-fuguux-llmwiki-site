# Involving Customers with Iterative Design

Chapter 4 of *The Design of Sites*. Covers the second pillar of [[customer-centered-design]] — keeping customers involved throughout design via [[iterative-design]] — and its three constituent activities: setting goals and designing, [[rapid-prototyping]], and [[usability-evaluation-methods|evaluating]] designs with real customers.

## Key points

- **Why iterate** ([[iterative-design]]): no design team has complete information about customer needs, and errors caught late in a project cost roughly 10x more to fix than if caught early, ballooning to 100–1000x after deployment. A 2x2 "right feature × right implementation" framework explains the distinct kinds of design mistakes iteration helps catch.
- **Design goals and principles**: goals (faster task completion, fewer errors, higher conversion, etc.) come from business/customer analysis ([[know-your-customers]]); seven design principles, adapted from Shneiderman, Nielsen, and Tufte, guide how patterns get applied to reach those goals — covered in depth in [[interface-design-principles]].
- **Rapid prototyping** ([[rapid-prototyping]]): site maps, storyboards, and schematics as early design artifacts; a fidelity spectrum from low-fi paper sketches through medium-fi to high-fi HTML mockups; horizontal (broad, shallow) vs. vertical (narrow, deep) prototypes; explicit advice to avoid computer-based design tools until structural issues are settled.
- **Evaluation** ([[usability-evaluation-methods]]): three complementary techniques — expert/heuristic review, informal qualitative evaluation (think-aloud protocol, critical incidents), and formal quantitative usability studies (bottom-line data) — used in combination across the project lifecycle rather than as alternatives.
- The chapter explicitly frames itself as the second of two pillars of customer-centered design introduced in Chapter 3, closing the loop opened by [[know-your-customers]].
