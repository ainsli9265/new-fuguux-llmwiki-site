# Usability Engineering (Wikipedia)

Wikipedia's article on usability engineering, the professional discipline that studies and tests the usability of interactive systems, distinct from (though closely allied with) UX and interaction design.

## Key points

- **Definition.** Usability engineering draws on computer science and psychology to identify usability problems, rather than to design solutions for them — a usability engineer's focus is the research/testing phase; finding the actual fix is the job of a UX or interaction designer.
- **History.** In 1988, John Whiteside (Digital Equipment Corporation) and John Bennett (IBM) published work isolating three key activities: early goal-setting, iterative evaluation, and prototyping. Jakob Nielsen's 1993 book *Usability Engineering* became the field's defining text, describing methods for testing throughout a product's development — checking for barriers to **learnability, efficiency, memorability, error-free use, and subjective satisfaction** before launch — and coining the term "usability engineering lifecycle."
- **International standards.** ISO 9241 part 11 defines usability operationally as the context, efficiency, and satisfaction with which specified users can perform tasks. NIST's Common Industry Specification for Usability and biometrics usability specs apply the same idea in U.S. industry contexts. (The article's accessibility-standards bullet list — WAI, Section 508, ADA, IBM's guidelines — duplicates ground already covered in this wiki's [[accessibility]] and [[wai]] pages.)
- **Errors: slips vs. mistakes.** A two-part taxonomy of human error: a **slip** is the right goal executed via the wrong action (a typo, clicking the wrong menu item — an automatic-behavior failure); a **mistake** is a consciously deliberated but wrong goal in the first place. A **mode error** is a dangerous slip subtype where a user acts correctly for the mode they think they're in but is actually in a different one (e.g. a car in reverse instead of drive) — designers mitigate this with modeless interfaces or by requiring a continuous action to stay in a special mode (e.g. holding a key for Photoshop's lasso tool).
- **Evaluation methods.** Lists card sorting, cognitive task analysis, cognitive walkthroughs, contextual inquiry, focus groups, heuristic evaluation, interviews, questionnaires, surveys, think-aloud protocol, and usability testing — all already covered in this wiki ([[customer-research-methods]], [[usability-evaluation-methods]], [[card-sorting]]) — plus one not yet mentioned: the **RITE method** (Rapid Iterative Testing and Evaluation), which fixes problems and reruns tests within the same study rather than waiting for a full round to finish.
- **Notable practitioners:** Deborah Mayhew, Donald Norman, Alan Cooper, Jakob Nielsen, John M. Carroll, Larry Constantine, Mary Beth Rosson, Steve Krug — named but not elaborated on in the article.
